Bonds have ‘priced in’ recession, says JP Morgan’s Mitchell

(Bloomberg) – Bob Michele, who has worked in the bond market for more than 40 years, predicts a 75 percent chance of a U.S. recession in the next 18 months, but thinks it has been absorbed.

“Clients are coming back to the bond market, especially corporate bonds,” Michele, chief investment officer at JPMorgan Chase & Co (NYSE: JPM) Asset Management, said Friday on Bloomberg Television’s “Wall Street Week. “It’s because they have renewed confidence in central banks.”

Central banks around the world are aggressively raising interest rates to curb stubborn inflation while trying to avoid plunging their economies into recession. This week the European Central Bank raised interest rates for the first time in more than a decade. A Bloomberg survey of 44 economists conducted between 15 July and 20 July found expectations that the Federal Reserve would raise rates again by 75 basis points next week before easing to 50 basis points in September.

Michel said that with recession expectations at 75 per cent, the market “is now fairly pricing in where they think the Fed should go. The Fed is in sync with that. We’re talking about a year-end federal funds rate of around 3.5 per cent.”

Erin Browne, a multi-asset strategy portfolio manager at Pacific Investment Management Co. told Bloomberg Television that stocks had their best week in a month but didn’t fully absorb next year’s recession. “The market is pricing in essentially stagnant growth,” she said. “I think it’s going to be negative growth.”

Despite a stronger close to the week, which pared the market’s losses for the year to about 17 per cent, stocks fell on Friday as disappointing results from social media companies and weak economic data fuelled fears of a recession.

“Look, there’s a low bar going into the income season,” Brown said. “In fact, a lot of what you’re hearing from businesses right now is that consumers are weakening, but you’re also starting to see business confidence weakening as well.”

A survey by S&P Global (NYSE: SPGI) showed further gains in US Treasuries, pushing the 10-year yield up to around 2.7 per cent, while global business activity deteriorated in July, underscoring concerns about a slowdown in the economy.

Browne said persistent inflation will continue to show up in second quarter earnings, “but what’s new is that you’re starting to see higher financing costs start to really have an impact as well.”

Both sides agreed on the short-term outlook for Europe, saying inflation will likely remain structurally high and that the EU will continue to struggle with soaring energy costs as a result of Russia’s war on Ukraine.

The ECB will do what it can to slow consumption, although “the ECB can only raise interest rates so high, maybe 1.5 per cent, 1.75 per cent, and that’s it,” Michel said.

“Ultimately we like the sovereign debt there, but we like Germany,” he said. “We don’t necessarily sell in Italy.”

Browne and Michele diverged in their expectations for the dollar, which retreated this week after hitting record highs last week.

While Browne said she expected the dollar to continue to strengthen, Michele said it had “done all it could”.
