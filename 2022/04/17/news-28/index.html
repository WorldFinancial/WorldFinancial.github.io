Musk and the Twitter fiasco

Created2022-04-17|Updated2023-08-03|Internet
|Post View:

Musk and Twitter are gaining attention in international markets because of the takeover issue.

On April 14, Tesla CEO Elon Musk made a buyout offer to Twitter to acquire it for $54.20 per share in cash, valuing it at about $43.4 billion overall.

Musk said he invested in Twitter because he believes it now has the potential to become a global platform for freedom of expression, and I believe freedom of expression is a social responsibility for a functioning democracy.

And he believes that Twitter will neither thrive nor meet the needs of society. So Twitter needs to transform itself into a private company.

So he offered to buy 100 percent of Twitter for $54.20 per share in cash.

As to whether the acquisition could be successful, Musk said at the TED conference on April 14 that he had a plan B if Twitter refused. And said the acquisition does not look at the earnings.

After Musk’s announcement, Vanguard Group, one of the asset management companies, announced that they had purchased 10.3% of Twitter, making them the largest shareholder.

Goldman Sachs, on the other hand, said that Musk’s offer of $54.20 per share for Twitter was too low.

Saudi Prince Al Waleed bin Talal Al Saud, an investor with a 5.2% stake in Twitter, also said on social media that Musk’s $54.20 offer was not close to Twitter’s intrinsic value and that he and investment firm Kingdom Holding Company (KHC), one of Twitter’s largest long-term shareholders, would reject the offer The company’s offer will be rejected.

Twitter has said it will review Elon Musk’s offer on the advice of Goldman Sachs and Wilson Sonsini Goodrich & Rosati. Musk’s plan is to buy Twitter for about 43.4 billion valuation cash.

In the face of some opposition, Musk initiated a vote called “Taking Twitter private at $54.20 a share should be up to shareholders, not the board. Musk said that it would be completely untenable if shareholders were not allowed to vote, and that shareholders, not the board, own the company.

Musk, who often influences markets and businesses with his call to action, seems to be laying out a lot more.

On March 27, a netizen asked Tesla CEO Elon Musk if he would consider creating a new social media platform that consists of open source algorithms where free speech is given the highest priority and propaganda is minimized. Musk responded by saying he was seriously considering the matter. Earlier Musk said that free speech is essential to the functioning of democracy and questioned whether Twitter strictly adheres to the principles of free speech.

Soon after, on April 5, Twitter announced that it would appoint Musk to its board of directors.

As a result of this, Twitter (TWTR.N) pulled up in a straight line before the market and is now up nearly 7%.

Musk holds 9.2% of Twitter’s shares. And the CEO of Twitter also said: Musk is both an avid believer and a strong critic of Twitter, which is exactly what we need on Twitter.

After the news was announced, Musk said he would make significant improvements to Twitter in the coming months and said he looked forward to working further with Parag Agrawal and the Twitter board.

A few days later, things reversed.

On April 11, the Twitter CEO said: Elon Musk has decided not to join our board. Musk is our largest shareholder and we will continue to take his advice.

Then, on April 13, Elon Musk was sued by a Twitter (NYSE: TWTR) shareholder for failing to disclose that he had purchased a significant amount of the company’s stock, which had an impact on the company’s share price. At this point it was already known that Musk held a 9.2 percent stake in Twitter.

Bloomberg analysis, Musk does not join the Twitter board or because he wants to buy more shares, Musk currently holds about 9% of the Twitter company, and according to the internal restrictions of the Twitter board, his shareholding can not exceed 14.9%, which means he can not get a say in the board, not only can not take over Twitter, but also can not have much influence on Twitter This means that he will not have a say in the board and will not only not be able to take over Twitter, but will also not be able to have much influence on the service because it is very difficult to influence the board’s product decisions. The Bloomberg report points out that Musk wants to buy and influence Twitter, and by giving up his board seat, Musk can buy more shares of Twitter Inc.

Musk’s desire to change Twitter as well as being bullish on Twitter seems to be related to the freedom advocated by cryptocurrencies. twitter, as a pro-cryptocurrency platform, has a large number of cryptocurrency users.

This brings with it the expectation of users of Twitter and labels such as free speech and decentralization.

On April 7, former Twitter CEO Jack Dorsey expressed his regret for Twitter. Although Dorsey has announced his resignation as CEO of Twitter, he feels guilty about the role Twitter has played in promoting a “centralized Internet”.

Several prominent figures have also spoken out about the decentralization of Twitter.

The NBA’s Dallas Mavericks owner Mark Cuban said on social media that many tech giants such as Google and Facebook are now on the phone with their antitrust lawyers asking if they can buy Twitter and get approval, and Twitter is on the phone with their lawyers asking who could be their buyer.

Mark Cuban says Twitter may not choose to sell, but will instead find someone more friendly to buy Elon Musk’s stock and then kick him out. For the crypto community, the decentralization supporters group should create a DAO to buy Twitter and in the future let Token holders vote on topic trends and identity verification on social media.

And, FTX founder SBF decentralized Twitter with some implementation ideas.

  1. Tweets are up-linked and encrypted: senders choose who has access to them. If likes and retweets are also on the chain, this will force some L1 to scale to hundreds of thousands or even millions of TPS, but eventually it will happen.

  2. Monetization comes in two forms: tweet level, where the underlying protocol charges per message; UI level, where it can be profitable or subsidized, e.g., ads can be displayed, profit from it, and use it to pay for network expenses. Each UI can have its own review policy, no longer is one person/company controlling speech.

  3. the following could be integrated into Twitter: tips/payments/monetization for content creators; NFTs/profiles/avatars; and DOGE.

A large group of freedom and cryptocurrency advocates are suggesting that Twitter move from web2 to web3, but the vast business system of the Twitter platform is bound to be a long process if it moves to full decentralization. But compared to the change in platform, the wealth created by Twitter at the hands of capital comes from the users, and whether this can be decentralized is perhaps what the users care about after their enthusiasm. It’s like asking Musk if he’s willing to give up his own interests to distribute the proceeds to users. I wonder what the outcome will be?
